Infinity¶

This tutorial uses the symbol $\infty$ in a few places, and it's not at all obvious how it behaves. It is after all bigger than any other number, and that's kind of weird. Some mathematicians say that infinity is not a number at all, and that's correct in a way because infinity behaves very differently than finite numbers. This chapter contains many examples of that.

Basic Calculations¶

Experienced mathematicians probably want me to mention that I'm talking about the extended real number line. There, I have mentioned it.

$$\begin{align}\infty + 5 = \infty\end{align}$$

Yes, that's correct. If you add any finite number to $\infty$, you'll just get $\infty$. You can think about it like this: if you have $\infty$ lines of code and you write more code, you still have $\infty$ lines of code...

$$\begin{align}\infty - 5 = \infty\end{align}$$

...or if you delete some code, you still have lots of code! In general, $\infty + x = \infty$ for any finite $x$, even if $x$ is negative.

Here are more calculations:

$$\begin{align}\infty \cdot 5 &= \infty \\ \frac \infty 5 &= \infty \cdot \frac 1 5 = \infty \\ \infty \cdot (-5) &= -(\infty \cdot 5) = -\infty \\ \frac{\infty}{-5} &= -\frac \infty 5 = -\infty\end{align}$$

In general, if $x$ is finite, $\infty \cdot x = \infty$ if $x > 0$ and $\infty \cdot x = -\infty$ if $x < 0$. We'll talk about $\infty \cdot 0$ in a moment.

Powers of infinity behave like this:

$$\begin{align}\infty^4 = \infty \cdot \infty \cdot \infty \cdot \infty = \infty\end{align}$$

This works with any positive exponent, like $\infty^{1/2} = \infty$ or even $\infty^\infty = \infty$. Later we'll learn that things like $x^{-4}$ can be defined as $\frac{1}{x^4}$, and that's why $\infty^x = 0$ for a negative $x$.

Calculations with infinity can also yield a finite value.

$$\begin{align}\frac 1 \infty = 0\end{align}$$

Or in general, for any finite $x$:

$$\begin{align}\frac x \infty = \frac 1 \infty \cdot x = 0 \cdot x = 0\end{align}$$

Limits with infinity¶

Let's say you want to know what happens to $x^2 - x$ if $x$ gets really big. Sometimes you can just plug in $x=\infty$ and you'll find out. Let's see if that works in our case.

$$\begin{align}\infty^2 - \infty = \infty - \infty\end{align}$$

So, what is $\infty - \infty$? Maybe it's 0? Maybe it's $\infty$ or $-\infty$? If we first rewrite things slightly and then plug in $x=\infty$ we get something that makes more sense:

$$\begin{align}& x^2-x = (x-1)x \\ & (\infty-1) \cdot \infty = \infty \cdot \infty = \infty\end{align}$$

So if $x$ gets very big, $x^2 - x$ will also get very big. A mathy way to say this is that the limit as $x$ goes to $\infty$ of $x^2 - x$ is $\infty$.

$$\begin{align}\lim_{x\to\infty} (x^2-x) = \lim_{x\to\infty} x(x-1) = (\infty-1) \cdot \infty = \infty\end{align}$$

Here $x\to\infty$ means "$x$ goes to infinity", or "$x$ approaches infinity" if you like fancy words. It does not mean $x=\infty$, it means that we want to know what happens if $x$ is very big. Practically this means that we can first treat $x$ as a finite number and then plug in $\infty$.

How about $\infty-\infty$ or $\infty/\infty$?¶

Based on the above stuff, you might be thinking that $\infty-\infty=\infty$, but this limit gives something very different:

$$\begin{align}\lim_{x\to\infty}(x-x) = \lim_{x\to\infty}0 = 0\end{align}$$

Or this:

$$\begin{align}\lim_{x\to\infty}(x-x^2) = \lim_{x\to\infty}x(1-x) = \infty\cdot(1-\infty) = \infty\cdot(-\infty) = -\infty^2 = -\infty\end{align}$$

As you can see, $\infty-\infty$ limits can output many different values. This is why $\infty-\infty$ is undefined, just like division by 0. Most programming languages know this too:

>>> float('infinity') - float('infinity')
nan

NaN is short for "Not a Number".

Here are some examples of other undefined things.

$\displaystyle\frac\infty\infty$¶

I'll divide top and bottom by $x$ in these examples. If you try to do e.g. $\frac{\infty}{2\infty}$ you get $\frac \infty \infty$.

$$\begin{align}\lim_{x\to\infty} \frac{x}{2x} = \lim_{x\to\infty} \frac 1 2 = \frac 1 2\end{align}$$$$\begin{align}\lim_{x\to\infty} \frac{5x}{x} = \lim_{x\to\infty} \frac 5 1 = 5\end{align}$$

$\displaystyle\frac 0 0$¶

Dividing something by a huge number gives a tiny number, so these limits are $0/0$ situations:

$$\begin{align}\lim_{x\to\infty} \frac{\ \frac{1}{2x}\ }{\ \frac{1}{x}\ } = \lim_{x\to\infty} \frac{\ \frac{1}{2x} \cdot 2x\ }{\ \frac{1}{x} \cdot 2x\ } = \lim_{x\to\infty} \frac{1}{2} = \frac 1 2\end{align}$$

$0 \cdot \infty$¶

This one is interesting because 0 multiplied by anything finite is 0, and infinity multiplied by anything nonzero is either $\infty$ or $-\infty$ (see above).

$$\begin{align}\lim_{x\to\infty}(0 \cdot x) = 0\end{align}$$$$\begin{align}\lim_{x\to\infty} \biggl(\ \underbrace{\frac 1 x}_{\to0} \cdot \overbrace{x}^{\to\infty}\ \biggr) = \lim_{x\to\infty} \frac x x = \lim_{x\to\infty} 1 = 1\end{align}$$$$\begin{align}\lim_{x\to\infty} \biggl(\ \underbrace{\frac 1 x}_{\to0} \cdot \overbrace{x^2}^{\to\infty}\ \biggr) = \lim_{x\to\infty} \frac{x^2}{x} = \lim_{x\to\infty} \frac x 1 = \frac \infty 1 = \infty\end{align}$$

$1^\infty$¶

You might be thinking that it makes sense to define $1^\infty = 1$ because $1^x=1$ for any finite $x$ (multiplying 1 by itself $x$ times is still 1). Even though this limit is 1...

$$\begin{align}\lim_{x\to\infty}1^x = \lim_{x\to\infty} 1 = 1\end{align}$$

...it doesn't mean that all $1^\infty$ limits are 1. Here's a famous example that we'll revisit later:

$$\begin{align}e = \lim_{n\to\infty} \left(1+\frac 1 n\right)^n = 2.718281828459...\end{align}$$

Mathematicians call this number $e$. The inside of the limit goes to 1 because $\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}\textstyle(1+\frac 1 n)=1+0=1$, but the whole thing does not go to 1. Just try this with your favorite programming language or calculator:

>>> n = 10000000000
>>> (1 + 1/n)**n
2.7182820532347876
>>> import math
>>> math.e
2.718281828459045

Here are more $1^\infty$ limits:

$$\begin{align}\lim_{n\to\infty} \left(1+\frac 1 n\right)^{2n} = \lim_{n\to\infty} \Biggl(\left(1+\frac 1 n\right)^n\Biggr)^2 = e^2 = 7.389055...\end{align}$$$$\begin{align}\lim_{n\to\infty} \left(1+\frac 1 n\right)^{n\ \cdot\ n} = \lim_{n\to\infty} \Biggl(\left(1+\frac 1 n\right)^n\Biggr)^n = e^\infty = \infty\end{align}$$

$0^0$¶

Let's start by thinking about what $x^0$ is with a finite $x$. We know that $x^{a+b}=x^ax^b$ (see the basics page), so we can plug in e.g. $a=1$ and $b=0$:

$$\begin{align}x^{1+0} &= x^1x^0 \\ x &= x \cdot x^0\end{align}$$

This is true if $x=0$, and if $x \ne 0$, we can divide by $x$ on both sides to get $1=x^0$. So here's our conclusion: $x^0=1$ for any finite $x$ except $x=0$ and we know nothing about $0^0$ yet.

On the other hand, $0^x=0$ with any finite $x$, so we get these limits:

$$\begin{align}& \lim_{x\to0} 0^x = 0 \\ & \lim_{x\to0} x^0 = 1\end{align}$$

$\infty^0$¶

We just learned that $x^0=1$ with any finite $x$...

$$\begin{align}\lim_{x\to\infty}x^0=1\end{align}$$

...but we also have $\infty^x=\infty$ for any positive $x$...

$$\begin{align}\lim_{x\to0^+}\infty^x = \infty\end{align}$$

...and $\infty^x=0$ for a negative $x$:

$$\begin{align}\lim_{x\to0^-}\infty^x = 0\end{align}$$

Here $x\to0^+$ means that $x$ goes to 0 from the positive side; that is, we want to know what happens if $x$ is something like $0.00000001$. Similarly, $x\to0^-$ means approaching from the negative side. A plain $x\to0$ means that approaching from either side gives the same value. As a special case, $x\to\infty$ means $x\to\infty^-$ because it's not possible to approach $\infty$ from the positive side.
